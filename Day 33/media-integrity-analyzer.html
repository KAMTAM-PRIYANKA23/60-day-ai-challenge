● Case File No. 01

Headline Detective

Headlines are written to be clicked, not necessarily to be accurate. Publishers know that words like “shocking,” “secretly,” or “proves” earn more clicks than careful, honest ones — even when the actual story is far more ordinary.

The concept

A headline can be technically connected to a story while still misrepresenting it — through exaggeration, missing context, or loaded words that imply something the facts don't support.

This matters because most people only read the headline. If the headline lies, the misinformation spreads even when the article underneath is careful and accurate.

What you'll do

You'll see a headline and its article. Decide honestly whether you'd click it, then tap any words or phrases in the headline that feel exaggerated or misleading. We'll reveal the accuracy score right after.

● Case File No. 02

Emotion Detector

Some posts aren't trying to inform you at all — they're trying to make you feel something fast enough that you share, comment, or buy before you think it through.

The concept

Fear, anger, and urgency spread faster than calm explanation, because strong emotion short-circuits the pause where we'd normally ask “wait, is this true?”

This matters because algorithms reward whatever keeps you engaged — and outrage or fear keeps you engaged very effectively. Recognizing the trigger is the first step to not being steered by it.

What you'll do

You'll read a short post or caption. Tell us how it made you feel, then tap the specific words that influenced that feeling. We'll reveal who it was really built for, and how.
